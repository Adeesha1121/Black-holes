Black Holes: Everything You Need to Know

Black holes are some of the strangest and most fascinating objects in space. They're extremely dense, with such strong gravitational attraction that not even light can escape their grasp.

Discovery

The first image of a black hole was captured in 2019 by the Event Horizon Telescope (EHT) collaboration. The striking photo of the black hole at the center of the M87 galaxy 55 million light-years from Earth thrilled scientists around the world.

How Many Black Holes Are There?

The Milky Way could contain over 100 million black holes, though detecting these gluttonous beasts is very difficult. At the heart of the Milky Way lies a supermassive black hole — Sagittarius A*. The colossal structure is about 4 million times the mass of the sun and lies approximately 26,000 light-years away from Earth, according to a statement from NASA.

WHAT DO BLACK HOLES LOOK LIKE?

Black holes have three main components: the outer and inner event horizon, and the singularity. The event horizon marks the boundary beyond which light cannot escape, and gravity is constant across this boundary. The singularity is the central point in space-time where the mass of the black hole is concentrated.

Observing black holes is challenging because they do not emit light directly. Instead, scientists rely on detecting radiation emitted as surrounding dust and gas are pulled into the black hole. Supermassive black holes at a galaxy's center may be obscured by thick surrounding material, hindering observations.

As matter falls toward a black hole, some particles can be ejected outward, creating bright jets of material moving at high speeds. These jets are observable even though the black hole itself remains unseen.

The Event Horizon Telescope (EHT) captured an image of a black hole in M87 in 2019, a remarkable achievement that required extensive collaboration among telescopes worldwide. Future efforts aim to image more black holes, with Sagittarius A* at the center of the Milky Way as a potential target. This black hole is of interest due to its unexpectedly quiet nature, possibly influenced by magnetic fields, and the presence of a cool gas halo around it, providing valuable insights into the surroundings of a black hole.

Types of Black Holes

Stellar black holes — small but deadly

When a large star exhausts its fuel, it collapses to form a stellar black hole. These black holes, created by the collapse of individual stars, are small but extremely dense. With over three times the mass of the sun packed into a city-sized diameter, they exert powerful gravitational forces, pulling nearby objects toward them. Stellar black holes continuously grow by consuming dust and gas from their surrounding galaxies.

Supermassive black holes — the birth of giants

Supermassive black holes, much larger than their smaller counterparts, are believed to exist at the center of nearly every galaxy, including the Milky Way. These giants, millions or billions of times more massive than the sun, have a comparable diameter. The origin of supermassive black holes is not fully understood, and several theories exist.

One possibility is the merger of hundreds or thousands of smaller black holes, contributing to the formation of a supermassive black hole. Alternatively, large gas clouds collapsing and rapidly accumulating mass could play a role. Another hypothesis involves the collapse of a stellar cluster, where a group of stars falls together, forming a supermassive black hole. Lastly, large clusters of dark matter, a mysterious substance with gravitational effects but no direct observation, might contribute to the creation of supermassive black holes. Once formed, these black holes continue to grow by accumulating mass from the abundant dust and gas in the central regions of galaxies.

Intermediate black holes

Intermediate black holes (IMBHs), once thought to be only in small and large sizes, are now considered a possibility. These midsize black holes could form through chain reactions of star collisions within a cluster. If multiple IMBHs form in the same region, they might eventually merge at the center of a galaxy, creating a supermassive black hole.

In 2014, astronomers discovered what seemed to be an intermediate-mass black hole in a spiral galaxy's arm, and in 2021, they detected one using an ancient gamma-ray burst. Research from 2018 suggested that IMBHs might exist in the hearts of dwarf galaxies. Observations of 10 such galaxies revealed X-ray activity, a common trait of black holes, indicating the presence of black holes ranging from 36,000 to 316,000 solar masses. This information was gathered from the Sloan Digital Sky Survey, which examines about 1 million galaxies and can detect the light emitted by black holes as they capture nearby debris. The discovery of IMBHs challenges previous assumptions about black hole sizes and distribution in the universe.

Binary black holes: double trouble

In 2015, astronomers using the Laser Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory (LIGO) made a groundbreaking discovery by detecting gravitational waves from the merger of stellar black holes. This confirmed the existence of stellar-mass black holes larger than 20 solar masses, challenging previous knowledge. The observations also provided insights into the spin directions of merging black holes, revealing that they can either spin in the same or opposite directions.

Two theories explain the formation of binary black holes. The first suggests that two black holes in a binary system form simultaneously from two stars born and explosively dying at the same time. The second proposes that black holes in a stellar cluster pair up after sinking to the cluster's center. LIGO's observations of companion black holes with different spin orientations support the latter theory, providing valuable data for understanding the formation of binary black hole systems. This research contributes to narrowing down favored models of black hole binary formation and improving our understanding of these cosmic phenomena.

Black Hole FAQs Answered by an Expert

How do black holes form?

Black holes can form through two primary pathways. The first involves the death of massive stars, typically those with a birth mass of 8 to 10 times that of our sun. When these stars exhaust their hydrogen fuel, they explode, leaving behind a compact, dense object known as a stellar mass black hole, with a mass a few times that of the sun. Stars with lower birth masses may result in neutron stars or white dwarfs instead.

The second pathway involves the direct collapse of gas, leading to the formation of more massive black holes. These black holes can range from 1000 to 100,000 times the mass of the sun. This process, occurring without the traditional formation of a star, is believed to be prominent in the early universe and produces larger black hole seeds.

Who discovered black holes?

Black holes were predicted as an exact mathematical solution to Einstein's equations. Einstein's equations describe the shape of space around matter. The theory of general relativity connects the geometry or shape of shape to the detailed distribution of matter.

The black hole solution was found was by Karl Schwarzschild in 1915, and these regions — black holes — were found to distort space extremally and generate a puncture in the fabric of spacetime. It was unclear at the time if these corresponded to real objects in the universe. Over time, as other end products of stellar death were detected, namely, neutron stars seen as pulsars it became clear that black holes were real and ought to exist. The first detected black hole was Cygnus-X1.

Do black holes die?

Black holes are not expected to die in the conventional sense, but a theoretical prediction by Hawking suggests they may slowly evaporate over extremely long time scales. Black holes grow by pulling in nearby matter due to their immense gravity, but Hawking proposed that they could also radiate away energy, gradually shrinking. In the realm of quantum theory, virtual particle-antiparticle pairs constantly appear and disappear. When this occurs near a black hole's event horizon, one of the particles may fall into the black hole, while the other escapes into space. Over immensely extended time frames, much longer than the age of the universe, this process is theorized to cause the black hole to slowly evaporate.

Are black holes wormholes?

Wormholes are distinct from black holes. While black holes are gravitational singularities, wormholes can be envisioned as tunnels connecting different points in space and time. Some theories suggest that the interior of black holes might contain wormholes, serving as portals to other locations or even different universes.

Black Hole Facts

  • If you fell into a black hole, theory has long suggested that gravity would stretch you out like spaghetti, though your death would come before you reached the singularity. But a 2012 study published in the journal Nature suggested that quantum effects would cause the event horizon to act much like a wall of fire, which would instantly burn you to death.

  • Black holes don't suck. Suction is caused by pulling something into a vacuum, which the massive black hole definitely is not. Instead, objects fall into them just as they fall toward anything that exerts gravity, like the Earth.

  • The first object considered to be a black hole is Cygnus X-1. Cygnus X-1 was the subject of a 1974 friendly wager between Stephen Hawking and fellow physicist Kip Thorne, with Hawking betting that the source was not a black hole. In 1990, Hawking conceded defeat.

  • Miniature black holes may have formed immediately after the Big Bang. Rapidly expanding space may have squeezed some regions into tiny, dense black holes less massive than the sun.

  • If a star passes too close to a black hole, the star can be torn apart.

  • Astronomers estimate that the Milky Way has anywhere from 10 million to 1 billion stellar black holes, with masses roughly three times that of the sun.

  • Black holes remain terrific fodder for science fiction books and movies. Check out the movie "Interstellar," which relied heavily on Thorne to incorporate science. Thorne's work with the movie's special effects team led to scientists' improved understanding of how distant stars might appear when seen near a fast-spinning black hole.
